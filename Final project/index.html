  • Nigunim
    • Born: Rabbi Menachem Mendel Schneerson was born on the 11th of Nissan, 1902, in Nikolaev, Ukraine. Early Life: He came from a distinguished Chabad family, with deep Torah knowledge and leadership.
    • Education: Studied in local yeshivas and later in Berlin and Paris, excelling in secular and Jewish studies.
    • Marriage: In 1928, he married Chaya Mushka Schneerson, daughter of the sixth Lubavitcher Rebbe, Rabbi Yosef Yitzchak Schneersohn.
    • Arrival in U.S.: In 1941, he immigrated to the United States, escaping Nazi persecution, and began assisting his father-in-law.
    • Leadership Role: Upon the passing of his father-in-law in 1950, he became the seventh Rebbe of the Chabad-Lubavitch movement.
    • Vision: The Rebbe's vision was to spread Judaism through education, outreach, and community building. Chabad Expansion: Under his leadership, Chabad grew rapidly, with hundreds of centers worldwide.
    • Tefillin Campaign: Launched in the 1960s, the campaign encouraged Jewish men to observe the mitzvah of tefillin.
    • Shluchim Program: Established a global network of emissaries to strengthen Jewish identity and community.
    • Mitzvah Campaigns: Initiated campaigns to promote Jewish observance, including Torah study, Shabbat, and kosher food.
    • Outreach Efforts: Focused on reaching out to Jews from all walks of life, even those far removed from traditional Judaism.
    • Moshiach Belief: The Rebbe emphasized the belief in the imminent arrival of the Messiah and worked to bring that reality closer.
    • Jewish Education: Founded numerous schools, yeshivas, and institutions, laying the groundwork for a worldwide Jewish revival.
    • Philanthropy: The Rebbe supported and encouraged charitable endeavors, providing for the physical and spiritual needs of Jews.
    • Advocacy for Jewish Rights: Advocated for the preservation of Jewish rights and values in both the United States and Israel.
    • Passing: The Rebbe passed away on the 3rd of Tammuz, 1994, leaving an indelible mark on Jewish life worldwide.
    • Legacy: His teachings, writings, and leadership continue to inspire millions, ensuring his vision lives on.
    • Global Influence: Chabad today remains one of the largest and most influential Jewish movements, with over 5,000 emissary families worldwide.
    Light Shabbat Candles

    Women and girls (age 3 and up) are encouraged to light candles every Friday afternoon, 18 minutes before sunset, in honor of the Shabbat, and before Festivals.

    Tefillin

    Men (age 13 and up) are encouraged to wear the Tefillin every morning excluding Shabbat and Festivals. Tefillin are black leather boxes containing small parchment scrolls of selected portions from the Torah, in which the fundamentals of the Jewish faith are inscribed.

    Mezuzah

    Every Jewish home should have a mezuzah on its doorposts. The mezuzah contains the Shema and is a sign that the home is sanctified for G‑d and enjoys His protection.

    Torah

    Study a portion of Torah daily. Even a few lines contain the infinite wisdom and will of G‑d.

    Tzedakah

    Give charity daily. When you give to the needy, you are serving as G‑d's emissary to provide for His creations. The home is a classroom, and keeping a"pushkah" (charity box) inyour home — and contributing a coin to it every day — will teach you and your children the noble value of regular giving.

    A home filled with holy books

    Furnish your home with as many holy books as possible. At the very least, get a hold of a Chumash (Bible), Psalms, and a Prayer Book.

    Kashrut

    Eating is one of the basics of life. Shouldn't it be done with intelligence? For a healthy and sound soul, eat only kosher foods, for when you eat differently, your Judaism is not just metaphysical, but part and parcel of your very being.

    Love Your Fellow

    "Love your fellow as youself," said the great Jewish sage Rabbi Akiva, is a most basic principle in the Torah. Reaching out to your fellow Jew with patience, love, concern and unity is among the greatest mitzvot a Jew can do.

    Education

    Every Jewish boy and girl should receive a Jewish education. Teach your children everything you know about your faith, and provide them with a quality Jewish education — you will be ensuring Jewish integrity, Jewish identity and a Jewish future.

    Observe Family Purity

    Observance of the Jewish marital laws allows you to make the most of your marriage, bringing you and your spouse to new, undiscovered depths of intimacy and sacredness in your relationship.
